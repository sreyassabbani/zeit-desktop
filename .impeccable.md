## Design Context

### Users

The primary user is an easily-overloaded individual organizing personal work, appointments, focus time, and reminders on a Mac. They live in the week view, scan the month for shape, and need the calendar to reduce decisions rather than become another inbox. Success means they can understand today in seconds, move through time without friction, and trust small surfaces such as widgets to show the right event.

### Brand Personality

Calm, exact, quietly opinionated.

### Aesthetic Direction

Use Notion Calendar's density and direct manipulation as an interaction reference, not a visual skin to clone. The app should feel native to macOS: system typography, compact controls, a hidden-inset titlebar, neutral surfaces, fine separators, one restrained indigo accent, and excellent light/dark appearance. Avoid generic dashboard cards, gradients, glass effects, oversized headings, and decorative illustration. The calendar grid should own the window.

### Design Principles

1. Calendar first: chrome and sidebars serve the time grid and never compete with it.
2. Compact, not cramped: use a 4-point rhythm, strong alignment, and few type sizes.
3. Attention is a feature: surface ranking is explicit, inspectable, and user-overridable.
4. Native performance is visible: OS scrolling, stable layout, bounded view trees, and immediate input feedback.
5. Progressive disclosure: common navigation is always present; provider and rule complexity appears only when asked for.
6. Accessible by construction: keyboard parity, native focus rings, AA contrast, non-color state cues, and reduced-motion behavior.
